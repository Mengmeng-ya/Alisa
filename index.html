New Zealand Introduction ▼
New Zealand, a sovereign nation in the southwest Pacific, comprises the North and South Islands plus smaller isles. Covering ~270,000 km², it boasts a temperate maritime climate and stunning landscapes—from snow-capped peaks and glacial fiords to lush rainforests. With English and Māori as official languages, it blends indigenous culture with modernity, renowned for pristine nature, outdoor adventures, and high-quality dairy exports. Auckland (largest city) and Wellington (capital) are key hubs, making it a beloved destination for nature lovers worldwide.
Brief History of New Zealand ▼
Early Settlement：Around 950 CE, Polynesian navigator Kupe first arrived, naming the land "Aotearoa". By 1350 CE, large groups of Māori migrated and settled, forming tribal societies centered on agriculture and fishing, with distinct crafts and hierarchical systems (chiefs, free people, slaves).
European Contact & Colonization：Dutch explorer Abel Tasman visited and named it "New Zealand" in 1642, but no large-scale colonization followed. After British Captain James Cook mapped the islands in 1769, European immigrants (whalers, missionaries, traders) flocked in. The Treaty of Waitangi was signed in 1840, making New Zealand a British colony. Subsequent land disputes led to a 30-year Māori uprising (1843–1872).
Development & Self-Governance：In the late 19th century, gold discoveries and immigration boosted economic growth, with animal husbandry becoming a core industry. It became a British dominion in 1907, gained independence in domestic and foreign affairs in 1931, and fully adopted the Statute of Westminster in 1947, achieving complete self-governance while remaining in the Commonwealth.
Modern Era：New Zealand fought alongside Britain in both World Wars. Post-WWII, it shifted to cooperation with the US. Its economy faced challenges from Britain’s entry into the EEC and the oil crisis, later stabilizing gradually. It established diplomatic relations with China in 1972, withdrew from the ANZUS Treaty in 1987, and continues to promote multiculturalism and Māori rights protection.
